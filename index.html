Freedom of Information Casework
First‑tier Tribunal

Case
study
← Back to portfolio

Overview

A self‑directed FOIA case that progressed through internal review, ICO complaint, and ultimately to the First‑tier Tribunal, argued without legal representation.

The request sought records of the 9 April 2022 Kyiv visit. The Cabinet Office refused, relying on a combination of national security, international relations, and law enforcement exemptions. This case provided a ground‑level view of how the UK’s information rights system operates in practice.

Procedural history

  • 2023
    Initial request & refusal
    Request submitted; refused by Cabinet Office citing ss.23, 24, 26, 27, 35, 38 and 40.
  • 2024
    Internal review & ICO complaint
    Internal review upheld the refusal; complaint filed with the Information Commissioner’s Office.
  • 2025
    First‑tier Tribunal appeal
    Appeal lodged, grounds prepared without counsel. Tribunal stage ongoing.

Key lessons

The case highlighted the tension between the public interest test and the government’s broad interpretation of national security exemptions. It also demonstrated the procedural challenges faced by individual applicants navigating the tribunal system without legal support.

This work continues to inform my understanding of institutional transparency and the limits of disclosure regimes.

Project scope

An eighteen‑month reconstruction of negotiation timelines from primary and open sources, 2023–2025, produced for the research project Listening to the Other Side.

The chronology maps official statements, meeting records, and back‑channel communications across multiple languages, with a focus on verifiability and source transparency.

Methodology

  • Sourcing
    Multilingual OSINT
    Russian, Ukrainian, English, and French sources – official transcripts, leak‑based reporting, and social media.
  • Verification
    Cross‑referencing
    Each entry corroborated against at least two independent primary or secondary sources.
  • Output
    Timeline & analysis
    Interactive timeline and written narrative used for academic and media briefings.

Key findings

The chronology revealed patterns of miscommunication and missed windows for de‑escalation, particularly in the early months of 2024. It also highlighted the role of intermediary states and non‑official channels in sustaining dialogue even during active hostilities.

This work underscored the importance of disciplined source handling in conflict‑zone research and the value of long‑term diplomatic record‑keeping.

File W/01 / Working paper

The China dependence paradox
in Zimbabwe's minerals policy

Analysis
← Back to writing

Argument

Zimbabwe's strategic advantage in the lithium and platinum sectors lies not in its resource endowment, but in its capacity to sequence policy reforms ahead of competitor states.

While much of the literature focuses on Zimbabwe's mineral wealth, this paper argues that the timing of regulatory changes, investment treaties, and local content requirements matters more than the volume of reserves. Harare's ability to move first on export restrictions and ownership frameworks gives it a unique form of leverage over Chinese state-owned enterprises.

Policy sequencing

The paper traces three policy windows: the 2022 lithium export ban, the 2023 local processing requirements, and the 2024 fiscal stabilisation measures. Each step was designed to capture downstream value before Chinese firms could lock in low-cost extraction models.

By contrast, other African states with larger reserves have struggled to implement similar policies due to bureaucratic inertia or donor conditionality. Zimbabwe's relative isolation has, in this instance, enabled a degree of policy agility.

Implications

For investors, the lesson is to track policy signals rather than geological surveys. For regional peers, the case offers a template for leveraging dependence without provoking capital flight. The paradox is that Zimbabwe's weakness—its exclusion from Western financial systems—has become a source of bargaining strength in its engagement with Beijing.

File W/02 / Essay

What an FOI refusal
teaches you about the state

Essay
← Back to writing

Beyond the black box

A refusal is not merely a withheld document—it is a map of the state's priorities, insecurities, and institutional culture.

This essay draws on my own FOIA casework to analyse how exemption reasoning reveals more than it conceals. The language used in refusal letters, the choice of exemptions, and the structure of the public interest test all offer clues about how officials perceive risk and accountability.

Exemption logic

Sections 23 (security bodies), 24 (national security), and 27 (international relations) are often deployed as a catch-all, but their application is rarely uniform. In my case, the Cabinet Office relied on a mix of substantive and procedural grounds, suggesting internal disagreement about how to handle the request.

Reading the refusal alongside the internal review and ICO decision, it becomes clear that the state is not a monolith—it is a set of competing interests, and disclosure requests expose those fractures.

What I learned

The process taught me to read administrative language as a source of intelligence. The gaps, the shifting justifications, and the occasional admissions of 'information held but withheld' all build a picture of institutional behaviour that is more valuable than the withheld records themselves.

File W/03 / Research note

Reading the April 2022
Kyiv record

Note
← Back to writing

Sourcing note

The diplomatic record of early Russia‑Ukraine negotiations is fragmentary, multilingual, and often contradictory. This note outlines the methodology used to reconstruct the April 2022 timeline.

Sources include Ukrainian parliamentary transcripts, Russian state media statements, Turkish mediation records, and leaked Western diplomatic cables. Each source carries its own bias, but triangulation across language groups yields a usable chronology.

Key dates

  • 1 April
    Istanbul round
    Initial security guarantees discussed; Ukrainian side signals potential neutrality.
  • 9 April
    Bucha aftermath
    Talks collapse as images of civilian deaths emerge; UK and US intensify sanctions.
  • 21 April
    Closed channel
    Private engagement via Israeli and Turkish intermediaries, with no public record.

Significance

The April window is critical because it represents the last moment before the war became entrenched. The available records suggest that both sides explored compromise, but external events—not just military dynamics—derailed the process. This note argues for a broader definition of the 'diplomatic record' that includes back‑channel communications and media silences.

File W/04 / Comparative brief

Indonesia's nickel ban
as a template, and its limits

Brief
← Back to writing

Template or trap?

Indonesia's export restriction on nickel has been widely cited as a model for Southern African critical minerals, but the context is not easily transferable.

This brief compares the Indonesian case with potential applications in South Africa, Zimbabwe, and Namibia, focusing on market concentration, domestic processing capacity, and geopolitical alignment.

Indonesia's success

Jakarta's ban on raw nickel exports—backed by aggressive downstream investment—has transformed its position in the global battery supply chain. Chinese firms responded by building smelters in Indonesia rather than losing market access, validating the state's bargaining power.

The key ingredients were: a near‑monopoly on high‑grade nickel, a unified political coalition, and a clear timeline for phasing in restrictions.

Southern African limits

Southern African states face structural constraints: fragmented producers, weaker state capacity, and greater donor dependence. A nickel or lithium ban in the region is likely to trigger immediate WTO challenges and capital flight, as seen in Namibia's 2023 rare‑earth proposals.

The brief concludes that a regional, coordinated approach—rather than a single‑state template—is more feasible, but requires trust and institutional alignment that currently does not exist.

File C/02 / Case file

Cabinet Office
disclosure case

FOI2023/11553
← Back to projects

Request

Submitted a Freedom of Information request to the Cabinet Office seeking records relating to the Prime Minister's visit to Kyiv on 9 April 2022. The request was refused under sections 23, 24, 26, 27, 35, 38, and 40 of the Freedom of Information Act 2000.

The request sought any communications, briefing materials, or situational reports prepared for or generated during the visit. The Cabinet Office refused the request in full, citing a broad range of exemptions.

Exemptions engaged

  • s.23
    Security bodies
    Information supplied by or relating to named intelligence and security agencies.
  • s.24
    National security
    Information whose disclosure would be prejudicial to the safety of the UK.
  • s.26
    Defence
    Information relating to the armed forces and military capabilities.
  • s.27
    International relations
    Information affecting UK relations with other states and international organisations.
  • s.35
    Government policy formulation
    Information relating to the development of policy and decision-making.
  • s.38
    Health and safety
    Information whose disclosure would endanger the physical or mental health of an individual.
  • s.40
    Personal information
    Information relating to an identifiable living individual.

Procedural status

Internal review upheld the refusal. An ICO complaint was filed and a decision notice issued. The case is now at the First‑tier Tribunal (Information Rights) stage, with grounds of appeal prepared without legal representation.

This project is the primary vehicle through which I am developing expertise in administrative law and institutional transparency.

File B/04 / Working paper

Risks Facing Chinese Mining Investments in Africa

Working
paper
← Back to portfolio

By Se Taylor • Geopolitical Risk Analysis • 2026

Introduction

China's engagement in the Sahel demonstrates a high tolerance for political and security risk. Chinese mining companies have become major players in the region's gold, uranium, and lithium sectors, often operating in fragile states where Western firms have scaled back. This paper examines the specific political, security, and operational risks that shape the investment environment for Chinese mining capital in the Sahel, with a focus on Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso.

Strategic Context

Beijing's strategic interest in the Sahel is driven by critical mineral demand, the Belt and Road Initiative's expansion into West Africa, and a desire to secure alternative supply chains outside of traditional Western-dominated markets. However, the region's recent wave of military coups—Mali (2020, 2021), Burkina Faso (2022), and Niger (2023)—has upended existing bilateral agreements, creating a volatile regulatory environment. Chinese state-owned enterprises (SOEs) and private firms alike must navigate shifting alliances, local hostility, and the growing influence of Russian mercenary groups.

Political Risks

Political risk is the most immediate threat to Chinese mining operations. Successive military juntas have renegotiated or threatened to revoke mining concessions, often citing "resource sovereignty" and demanding higher royalties or local ownership stakes. In Niger, the 2023 coup led to the suspension of uranium exports to France, but Chinese firms have been largely spared—so far. However, the precedent of contract renegotiation has introduced uncertainty. Bilateral investment treaties are inconsistently respected, and arbitration mechanisms are weak. Chinese firms often rely on diplomatic channels rather than legal recourse, a strategy that is effective but slow.

Security Risks

The Sahel is the epicentre of a sprawling insurgency linked to al-Qaeda and the Islamic State. Armed groups frequently target mining sites for extortion, kidnapping, and sabotage. Chinese workers and expatriate staff are increasingly vulnerable; in 2024, there were multiple incidents involving Chinese convoys near Ouagadougou. State security forces are overstretched and sometimes complicit in informal taxation. The withdrawal of French and UN peacekeeping forces from Mali has left a security vacuum that Chinese operations are ill-equipped to fill without significant private military contracts—a politically sensitive option.

Operational Risks

Operational risks include logistical bottlenecks (ports, roads, and rail), unreliable electricity supply, and a shortage of skilled local labour. Corruption and opaque procurement processes inflate costs and delay project timelines. Environmental regulations, where they exist, are weakly enforced, but this can backfire when local communities mobilise against pollution or land-use changes. Chinese firms have also faced difficulties with repatriating profits due to foreign exchange controls and sanctions (e.g., Niger and Mali are subject to limited EU/US restrictions).

Strategic Assessment

Chinese mining investments in the Sahel are not at immediate risk of collapse, but they operate in a high-risk, low-resilience environment. The key variables to watch are (1) the trajectory of military rule and its willingness to uphold existing contracts, (2) the intensity of insurgent violence near key mine sites, and (3) the degree of Chinese diplomatic engagement versus reliance on local proxies. Beijing is likely to adopt a flexible, case-by-case approach, accepting higher risk in exchange for exclusive access to strategic minerals. For investors and analysts, the Sahel remains a frontier market where political intelligence is as valuable as geological surveys.
